# Case Study: Drive Financial Acumen by Enabling Experiential Learning Among Non-Financial Decision Makers

By: Andrey Mozuliov

Initiatives: Talent Development and Financial Literacy

Decision makers hesitate to use financial modeling in planning because they are unfamiliar with how to use its outputs and insights. To drive a stronger decision-to-value loop in planning, FP&A and L&D leaders should leverage experiential simulation to improve financial literacy among non-financial leaders.

## Case Overview

### Problem

■ Despite the foundational importance of financial business acumen, many non-financial decision makers (e.g., product managers, marketers) are unfamiliar with how to use financial outputs because they don't fully understand the mechanics of the bottom line. Instead, they prefer familiar, siloed operational metrics.

■ Upskilling only finance teams on reading P&L and Cash Flow statements is insufficient because this concentrates financial knowledge in a few stakeholders. This concentration creates talent bottlenecks that make it impossible to address all of the organization's strategic planning needs.

### Action

■ **Immersive Safe-to-Fail Environment:** Organizations deploy "VR Nexus: Silicon & Senses," a competitive corporate strategy simulation that serves as an experiential learning platform to forge theoretical knowledge deep into participants' minds.

■ **Real-Time Financial Translation:** Companies utilize the simulation's engine to automatically translate participants' operational decisions (e.g., pricing, CapEx, R&D) into professional-grade financial statements, explaining the consequences of business decisions to stakeholders.

■ **Cross-Functional Friendly Competition:** Businesses prove the efficacy of financial rigor in planning to the entire organization by setting up high-visibility "War Room" competitions between cross-functional teams and AI competitors to maximize shareholder equity.

### Results

■ The number of non-financial staff able to comfortably navigate and use financial statements quadruples over the course of the training program, without adding any finance FTEs, and financially vetted project proposals increase significantly.

■ Decision makers embrace financial rigor in operational planning, viewing ROI and cash flow impact as the default criteria for processes with resource allocation needs.

## Problem

To navigate economic volatility, many organizations are turning to cross-functional leaders to increase their strategic planning agility. However, these implementations are commonly undercut by decision makers not fully understanding basic corporate finance. This impedes the decision-to-value loop, which is a proven driver of planning agility. Decision makers are unfamiliar with how to use the outputs of balance sheets, cash flow statements, and P&L statements during planning. Instead of rigorous financial modeling, they revert to traditional, siloed operational metrics they know and trust.

Organizations often believe they can address this unfamiliarity with finance by training decision makers on how it works using classroom lectures and static spreadsheets. However, this approach is insufficient and leads to a limited group of individuals with true financial intuition. This concentration of talent means any financially impactful questions or needs must pass through FP&A professionals. The resulting bottleneck delays addressing these planning needs and minimizes the potential impact of distributed, financially sound decision-making.

To address decision makers' unfamiliarity with finance, forward-thinking organizations improve financial application literacy among a broad group of non-financial managers from all functions. Multiplying the number of individuals who can address the organization's strategic needs increases broader business acumen. The idea sharing between leaders from all areas of the business also creates higher decision quality that strengthens the organization's trust in cross-functional planning and enables FP&A to increase financial rigor across the enterprise.

## Immersive Safe-to-Fail Environment

Many learning and development functions struggle to share complex financial knowledge with non-financial leaders because abstract concepts like depreciation, accumulated equity, and capital expenditures are difficult to internalize. This theoretical approach prevents leaders from building intuition around business cycles and limits their visibility into the impact of their decisions on long-term planning.

Recognizing the issue of static learning, companies deploy a dynamic, experiential simulation called "VR Nexus: Silicon & Senses." This platform immerses participants in the role of a hardware startup founder competing in a duopoly market over eight financial quarters. By tracking their decisions across R&D, production volume, and marketing in a continuous loop, the simulation enables participants to experiment with business strategies without real-world risk, effectively forging the theoretical knowledge deep in the participants' minds within a safe gaming environment.

Tracking this information in a centralized dashboard, "VR Nexus" enables participants to identify opportunities to collaborate with each other and aggregate individual decisions to address larger business workflows. For example, the simulation positions marketing leaders to improve their working capital forecasting by interacting directly with the accounts payable, accounts receivable, and inventory consequences of their brand spend. Tracking the results of each quarter makes it easy for non-financial leaders to understand the benefits of cash flow management and for executive leaders to see the collective value of financial literacy in planning.

## Real-Time Financial Translation

Increasing the amount of theoretical training does not, however, automatically increase decision makers' use of financial outputs for planning. Unfamiliar with how the mechanics work, decision makers still question why an aggressive revenue strategy results in a cash flow crisis, or why a stockout misses the mark. They seek answers to support their own decision-making comfort and to feel equipped to explain budget allocations to upward stakeholders. Without satisfactory answers to these questions, decision makers still resist using financial outputs to inform planning.

Cognizant of the need to fill this last-mile gap in financial education, the VR Nexus simulation serves as a real-time translator of operational decisions. The platform instantly translates pricing, capacity expansion, and R&D upgrades into professional-grade financial statements, leading to greater trust and adoption of financial principles in planning. 

The simulation's dashboard directs decision makers' questions to the engine's strict accounting rules (such as demonstrating the "Lag Rule" where investments take a full quarter to yield capacity). This translation ensures they have sufficient knowledge to understand the financial flow and explain it to stakeholders. The platform builds trust in the curriculum by visualizing the outcomes of market dynamics and how the simulation derives that output.

## Gamified Human-Machine Friendly Competition

To make the benefits of financial acumen clear to the organization and increase the adoption of ROI-driven modeling in planning, enterprises showcase the impact of the training across the firm. To draw maximal attention, they run a competition between teams utilizing distinct financial strategies, measured against rigorous AI competitors like "Apex Optic" and "ValueVirtua." The competition tests whether a cash-flow-first mindset or a high-debt growth strategy generates a higher Weighted Score in Total Equity and Market Share.

Running the simulation in front of senior leaders generates a lot of interest within the organization. Senior leader involvement also creates high visibility and draws employees from many parts of the firm into the competitive "War Room" debriefs.

Unsurprisingly, the teams that adopt rigorous cash-flow planning prove to be both more resilient and more efficient than those relying on traditional, siloed revenue goals. By positioning the learning journey as a friendly competition, organizations get both the observing staff and decision makers excited about the underlying mechanics. They can study live how a financially sound forecast leads to better results and learn from their peers about their experiences in using debt instruments versus organic growth.

The gamified friendly competition is a fun way of lessening stakeholders' defensiveness toward complex finance topics and getting the whole organization engaged.

## Results

Integrating simulations like VR Nexus into corporate training leads to widespread adoption and success with new business acumen. The number of non-financial decision makers trained in reading P&L and Cash Flow statements quadruples over the course of a standard training cycle. As a result, the number of financially vetted, ROI-positive project proposals within implementing organizations increases markedly.

This increased engagement from leaders with financial metrics in planning also makes decision makers more comfortable with complex terminology, leading to significant operational benefits. As participants consistently report, "Understanding the bottom-line impact of our operational choices in a simulated environment enables us to move faster and with greater confidence in the real world."

## Recommendations

L&D and FP&A leaders who want to get their organizations to engage with financial metrics in planning should:

■ Ensure non-financial decision makers have access to a robust experiential knowledge base by deploying interactive simulations like VR Nexus to practice the application of theoretical concepts without real-world risk.

■ Ensure decision makers actually understand financial data in planning by utilizing tools that automatically translate cross-functional operational decisions into concrete, professional-grade financial statements.

■ Create broad interest in financial acumen by hosting a demonstration of the training's efficacy in front of key stakeholders in the form of a friendly simulation competition ("War Room"), making it visible to the organization.

## Evidence

We developed this white paper to describe the approach to enabling greater financial literacy and business acumen among non-financial decision makers using the VR Nexus simulation platform. It is based on the experiential learning mechanics engineered to forge theoretical knowledge into applied operational planning across broad corporate training environments.
